# Business plan — foundation

Module 1 closed with a project that has direction, but with acknowledged gaps. Core hypotheses have not yet been tested with primary evidence, and part of what underpins the model rests on interviews with business owners who, in the end, were the wrong people to speak to about the pain points the product promises to solve. This chapter returns to the foundation more carefully, organising pain, segmentation, value proposition, business model, metrics, stakeholders and monetisation strategy as pieces of a plan that needs to support the demo build and the panel presentation.

I start with the pain, because everything depends on it.

---

## 1. Identified pain

Module 1 clarified whose pain this is. The initial intuition placed the business owner at the centre, but the three interviews from Sprint 2 showed that this discomfort, where it exists, is marginal. The payroll process works for most SMEs, sustained by accounting offices that deliver the service competently and at an acceptable cost.

The pain belongs to the CLT employee earning up to 3 minimum wages. There are three layers that need to be understood together.

The first is structural. Income is predictable, but the margin is zero. IBGE estimates that more than 60% of formally employed workers in Brazil earn up to 3 minimum wages — around 20 million people. A 2024 survey by CNDL and SPC Brasil showed that 6 out of 10 Brazilians would be unable to absorb a R$ 1,000 financial emergency without taking on debt. For this profile, rent, food, transport and dependants consume the salary before the end of the month.

The second layer is lack of access. Brazil has more than 70 million people with negative credit records at Serasa, concentrated in this income bracket. For a traditional bank, the credit score filter rejects the application before any analysis. Private payroll credit exists and would be the logical solution, but it requires a formal agreement between the employer and the bank — a process that most small SMEs have never implemented. What remains is what the market offers to those who have been excluded: informal lenders and apps charging between 10% and 15% per month. A R$ 300 need turns into a debt that erodes months of wages.

The third layer is the refused channel. The employer, who would be the natural solution, has neither the interest nor the structure to resolve it. Fernando Samogin recounted in the Module 1 interview that workers at his steel company asked for advances and he refused. It was not a lack of empathy. It was the rational decision of someone who understands that lending money to employees creates an employment and financial risk that the company is not equipped to manage. The demand exists, the worker needs it, the natural channel refuses, and the problem sits in a vacuum.

A caveat. This characterisation is based on three interviews with business owners (not with workers) and on robust secondary data. Primary evidence from those who actually feel the pain is missing. Module 2 will attempt to reduce this gap through parallel conversations with workers from the personal network, but part of the diagnosis still needs to be confirmed through the voice of those who live the problem.

There are two secondary pain points that matter for the channel model to work.

The SME that employs this worker suffers as a consequence. Employees under financial stress have lower productivity, take more sick days and resign due to financial pressure. Enzo Andrade described high turnover in the base-level operational roles of a franchisor, with causes that included low wages and no margin for unexpected expenses. The cost of this turnover is usually underestimated by the company itself, which frames the problem as "high turnover" without associating the financial cause.

The accounting office has a latent pain. The more than 102,000 active offices in Brazil already have the trust of SMEs and the payroll data that is exactly the input needed to originate payroll credit. But they operate on thin margins, depend on fixed fee revenue, and currently have no platform that turns this relationship into a new recurring revenue stream. The pain is not acute. It is latent: the office does not wake up thinking it needs another product, but it recognises value when someone shows up with something that increases revenue without increasing operational load.

Why has this pain, being measurable and having an obvious distribution channel, not been solved yet? Because the go-to-market model needed to serve small SMEs is not what the traditional market uses. Mêntore Bank requires the company to switch banks and hand over the entire payroll — an adoption barrier that is too high for small SMEs. The segment is ignored by structure, not by absence of pain.

This gap defines who Ágio needs to serve.

## 2. Customer segmentation

The segmentation reflects the model's structure. Forcing Ágio into a single segment impoverishes the analysis and hides what makes it different. There are three groups with distinct profiles, pain points and roles.

### Base-level CLT worker

Formally employed in the private sector, income between 1 and 3 minimum wages (R$ 1,518 to R$ 4,554 in 2025), between 20 and 45 years old, average education. Not concentrated in one region, but more easily addressable in state capitals and metropolitan areas due to the volume of formal companies.

Sectoral segmentation matters more than demographics. This worker is concentrated in manufacturing, retail, franchises, outsourcing, construction, transport and delivery — sectors with high turnover in base-level functions, no structured career path, and benefits limited to the legal minimum. Focusing acquisition on offices that serve these sectors is what makes the channel efficient.

No financial reserves, frequently with a negative credit record at Serasa, no access to traditional private payroll credit. When credit is needed, they turn to family members, informal lenders with high rates, revolving credit card debt, or loan sharks. Expected product usage is sporadic: medical emergency, overdue bill, medication, mobile phone top-up. Ticket between R$ 150 and R$ 300, projected frequency of 1.5 times per month for the active user — a figure borrowed from Mêntore Bank's documented operation.

This is the segment that pays for the product, and it is where the most important and still unvalidated hypothesis lives: with the product available, will they use it?

### Accounting office

Distribution channel and commercial partner. The 102,000 active offices in Brazil (CRCSP and CFC, January 2026), with 29,000 in São Paulo, serve between 50 and 200 companies on average. They operate predominantly on a fixed fee model, with thin margins and heavy dependence on a single source of revenue.

The profile that interests Ágio is the office whose client base consists predominantly of SMEs in the sectors where segment 1 is concentrated. Estimating that 20% of Brazilian offices fit this profile, there are around 20,000 addressable offices in the country and 6,000 in São Paulo. This segmentation still needs practical validation and will be adjusted as Sprint 4 conversations reveal which profile responds best.

Conservative behaviour. New client acquisition is done 95% through referrals (Panorama do Empreendedorismo Contábil 2023), which demonstrates consolidated trust with each client. Two sides for Ágio: activating a single office gives access to an entire portfolio with trust already established, but the office will resist anything that might shake that trust or increase operational load. The proposition needs to be simple, with zero risk for the office.

Financial relationship: a commission of some percentage on the revenue generated by the active portfolio, passed on monthly. No onboarding cost, no investment, no credit risk.

### SME with 10 to 100 employees

Served by an external accounting office. The SME is neither a client nor a commercial partner. It is an intermediary channel, and participates in the model at no cost, with no direct revenue, and without changing any process.

Why is it an intermediary channel and not a client? The cost of acquisition company by company makes the model unviable at this size for competitors that sell directly. Ágio resolves this impasse by accessing the company via the office, and the SME enters the model without anyone needing to sell to it directly. It agrees because the office, which it trusts, is presenting something that costs nothing and may reduce turnover.

The clear separation of the three segments underpins the model. Confusing the roles (selling directly to the company, or ignoring the accounting channel) is what prevents competitors' models from scaling to small SMEs.

## 3. Value proposition — Value Proposition Canvas

The value proposition structured in the Strategyzer Value Proposition Canvas appears in the document as a visual piece: one canvas per segment, organising the customer profile (jobs, pains, gains) and the value map (products and services, pain relievers, gain creators). The text below does not replace the canvas. It unpacks what each item means, justifies the choices and provides the context that the compact structure cannot accommodate.

The analysis below covers the CLT worker, the segment with the primary pain and the end customer of the financial product. The canvases for the other two segments (office and SME) are presented in sequence in the document, with analogous explanations.

### Jobs

The canvas lists jobs such as "cover an unexpected expense", "pay bills on time", "maintain financial dignity". Each sounds like a one-off need when viewed in isolation. The job that Ágio truly addresses is deeper: maintaining minimum financial functioning in a wage configuration that structurally cannot absorb unexpected expenses.

When a worker needs to cover a medical appointment before payday, they are not just settling that bill. They are trying to prevent a sequence: the bill becomes a debt, the debt becomes a negative credit record, the negative record blocks future opportunities. The job, at its core, is to interrupt the propagation of financial damage that a single unexpected expense can cause when there is no reserve.

The jobs-to-be-done framework works better for this segment if understood at two levels: functional job (cover the expense) and emotional/social job (maintain control, avoid exposure, preserve dignity). Ágio needs to deliver on both.

### Pains

The most obvious is the direct financial pain: high interest rates, bank rejection, abusive fees from informal lenders. But there are two adjacent pains that the canvas lists and that tend to be underestimated.

The first is embarrassment. Asking the boss for an advance exposes the financial situation to the employer. Asking family for a loan puts pressure on the relationship. Asking a loan shark involves risk and stigma. The path that avoids embarrassment (online lender with high interest) is exactly what creates the financial pain. The two pains reinforce each other.

The second is lack of predictability. Revolving credit card debt, instalment repayments and informal lenders have a cost that grows over time. The user enters a debt without knowing when it will end or how much they will ultimately pay. The unpredictability of the cost is a pain in itself, separate from the absolute value.

Ágio needs to address all three pains together. Solving only the financial pain without solving embarrassment and lack of predictability is not enough. That is why the product is digital, with no human interaction, and with a fixed fee per transaction.

### Gains

The gains listed in the canvas (access credit without a Serasa check, pay a fixed fee, handle it via mobile, have automatic deduction) appear to be functional benefits. But the most important gain, which does not fit in a single canvas line, is the sense of control. The worker who uses Ágio knows how much they will pay before requesting, knows when it will be deducted, and knows they will not accumulate debt without realising it.

The product's language reinforces this gain. By using "advance part of your salary" instead of "loan" or "credit", the product positions itself as something the worker does with their own money, not a debt they are taking on. This is a psychological distinction, not a legal one, but it has an impact on adoption. This language choice still needs to be tested with the real audience.

### Products and services

The canvas shows three main products: the web page accessed via link with CPF, the advance with automatic deduction from the next payroll, and the ticket between R$ 150 and R$ 300.

The web page without an app, without registration and without a password is a decision to reduce friction to a minimum. Apps require downloading, updating, phone storage — individually small barriers, but which add up for a demographic that uses a basic handset, with limited storage and a restricted data plan. The link with CPF resolves access and identification in a single step.

The automatic payroll deduction is the mechanism that makes the entire financial model viable. It is what keeps default rates close to zero (reference: 0.2% at Mêntore Bank with a similar mechanism) and what allows operating with a fee low enough not to become a trap. Without automatic deduction, the product becomes just another expensive credit app.

The ticket between R$ 150 and R$ 300 reflects the hypothesis about actual usage: one-off emergencies, not consumption financing. A larger ticket would increase the risk of the advance consuming a significant portion of the next paycheck. A smaller ticket would not cover most real emergencies. The current range is an estimate derived from documented behaviour in similar segments and will be adjusted as real data emerges.

### Pain relievers

The pain relievers listed in the canvas (no Serasa check, fixed fee, digital access, careful language, automatic deduction) directly address the pains listed. Each reliever connects to a specific pain.

There is a less obvious reliever: the automatic deduction prevents the accumulation of debt without the user noticing. For someone accustomed to revolving credit card debt, to a growing balance, to a lender that charges month after month, the idea of a transaction that closes itself and leaves no trail is in itself a relief. It operates more at the level of psychological security than financial security.

### Gain creators

The gain creators (resolved in a few clicks, predictable cost substantially lower than the informal market, the feeling of receiving a real benefit from the employer) are tied to the gains listed, but the third deserves a note.

When Ágio reaches the employee through the company's HR, via a WhatsApp link, it does not arrive as a standalone financial product. It arrives as a benefit offered by the company. The worker sees the product as something the employer made available, not as yet another credit app. The partial trust that exists in the worker-employer relationship is transferred to the product. This effect costs nothing (the company did not pay for Ágio), but it is a real gain creator, and it is one of the reasons the company channel works better than direct acquisition.

## 4. Business model

Ágio's model in one sentence: a platform that connects a basic financial product (salary advance) to an unexplored distribution channel (accounting offices), serving a segment ignored by capitalised players (SMEs with 10 to 100 employees). Each part of the sentence carries a choice.

The financial product is not new. Salary advance with automatic payroll deduction is a well-known model, validated at scale by Mêntore Bank in Brazil (R$ 1 billion in projected revenue for 2025, 0.2% default rate) and by DailyPay and EarnIn in the United States. The low default rate is not an accident. It derives from the deduction mechanism that happens before the money reaches the worker's account. It is the product's technical floor and what allows operating with a low fee without becoming a trap.

The distribution channel is where Ágio differentiates itself. The mapped competitors (Xerpa, Creditas, Flash, Caju, Bullla) operate through direct sales to companies. Mêntore Bank requires a bank switch. None of the players uses accounting offices.

The justification for the accounting channel is asymmetric. An average office serves between 50 and 200 SMEs. It has each business owner's trust, built over years. It already processes payroll monthly. It already has the salary data. By closing with one office, Ágio captures an entire portfolio at once. The acquisition cost per end company falls by an order of magnitude. The sales cycle shortens because the office, when it agrees to offer the product, becomes a recurring salesperson with trust already established.

This explains why the channel works in theory. Whether it works in practice for Ágio specifically is the question Module 2 needs to confront. The rational argument exists: a new recurring revenue stream on a portfolio the office already has, at no additional cost, without changing the payroll process. Correlated cases such as Contbank, which uses offices as partners for other financial products, support the viability. But practical confirmation only comes with the product in the hands of real accountants.

The target segment is SMEs with 10 to 100 employees, deliberately. Smaller companies do not have the volume to justify the office's operational effort. Larger companies fall within the radar of Mêntore Bank and the capitalised competitors. The 10-to-100 niche is what nobody serves well, and it is where employee pain is most evident, because the benefits package at these companies is more limited and the salary profile is predominantly base-level operational.

With product, channel and segment defined, the model operates in a cycle of four participants. Ágio provides the platform and captures the primary revenue. The office presents the product to the clients in its portfolio, authorises company onboarding and receives a percentage of revenue. The SME authorises the benefit for employees at no cost and no risk. The worker accesses it when needed, pays the fee per advance, and has the deduction applied automatically to the next payroll.

The flow of money follows a specific sequence. The office closes the month's payroll in whichever tool it uses (Domínio, Totvs, Senior). After closing, it exports the data as a CSV and uploads it to the Ágio panel. Ágio calculates the available limit for each employee (a percentage of net salary, with a reduced ceiling for employees with fewer than 3 months of tenure — identical logic to Mêntore's). The employee receives a link via WhatsApp from HR, accesses it when needed, requests the desired amount within the limit, and receives the money. At the following month's payroll closing, the office downloads the deduction report and posts each line in the payroll system like any other deduction. The cycle closes, the commission is passed on, and Ágio keeps the margin.

The current phase is validation, not full operation. In the MVP, Ágio operates with fictitious data for concept validation.

In the scale phase, the path is to structure a FIDC (Fundo de Investimento em Direitos Creditórios — Credit Rights Investment Fund) using the portfolio itself as collateral, with a licence from a financial institution such as QI Tech that provides the technological and regulatory infrastructure, without needing to seek authorisation from the central bank.

Pricing details, unit economics and cost structure come in Sprint 2. Revenue projections, break-even, IRR, NPV and ROE for 12 months come in Sprint 3. In-depth market and competitive analysis comes in Sprint 3. Hiring plan and pitch close in Sprint 4.

## 5. Metrics

Ágio's metrics fall into three groups: market (showing adoption speed and traction), financial (showing unit economics health) and operational (showing process fluidity). Seven indicators in total, formalised in Sprint 5 of Module 1 and revised here for Sprint 1 of Module 2.

### Market

**Active partner offices.** An active office is one with at least one registered company and at least 5 employees using the product. Target at the end of the validation phase: 3 offices. Warning signal: fewer than 1 active after 6 weeks of the minimum viable product phase. The number reveals the speed of channel adoption. If it stays low, the problem may lie in the commercial argument, the onboarding process or the proposition — and each diagnosis leads to a different correction.

**Employees with at least one advance in the month.** Calculated over the total registered employees. Target: above 40%. A low rate may indicate a failure in internal company communication, distrust or absence of need.

**Monthly usage frequency.** Average number of advances per active user per month. Target: 1.5 times. Warning signal: below 1.0 for three consecutive months. A user who uses the product once and does not return suggests it solved a one-off problem but did not create a habit. A user who uses it twice or more has made it part of their financial routine, which is the goal.

### Financial

**Net revenue per active user per month.** Shows how much Ágio retains per user after deducting the office commission. It is the indicator that determines whether the unit economics model works. The exact figure depends on three variables (fee per advance, average usage frequency and commission percentage) that still need to be finalised in Sprint 2. Tracking begins in the live operation phase, and the target will be defined once pricing is consolidated.

**Portfolio default rate.** Shows the robustness of the automatic payroll deduction mechanism. In similar models with direct payroll deduction, the default rate operates at low levels precisely because payment happens before the money reaches the worker's account. The exact target for Ágio will be calibrated as the first cycles run in live operation. Rising default rates indicate a failure in the deduction process, dismissal before payroll closing, or a communication breakdown with the office.

### Operational

**Time to onboard a new company.** Counted from the office's contract signature to the first advance completed. Target: less than 24 hours. Warning: above 72 hours. A slow process makes the office hesitate before recommending the product to the next clients in its portfolio.

**Employee satisfaction.** Measured by NPS after the first and second advance. Target: above 50. Warning: below 30. Shows whether the product resolves the pain in a way the user would recommend to a colleague. Below 30 indicates friction, a high perceived cost, or delivery below what was promised.

These seven indicators will not be measured in the Module 2 MVP demo, because the product operates with fictitious data. They formalise what will be measured in the live operation phase, and structure the execution plan presentation for the panel.

## 6. Stakeholder list

Ágio has five stakeholder groups, each with distinct interests, influence and roles. Clarity about who each one is and what they expect is part of what makes the model operable.

**CLT worker.** End customer of the product. Interest: access to emergency credit without friction and without exposure. Influence: high in the long term (usage or non-usage defines the model's viability), low in the short term (not the one who decides whether the product enters the company).

**Accounting office.** Distribution channel and commercial partner. Interest: new recurring revenue stream on an existing portfolio, without increased operational load. Influence: high. Without the office, the model does not scale. It is the stakeholder with effective veto power in the initial phase.

**SME (owner or HR).** Intermediary channel. Interest: offering a differentiated benefit to employees at no cost and no risk, reducing turnover in base-level functions. Influence: medium. Can accept or decline the product's entry into the company, but the presentation comes from the office, which it trusts.

**QI Tech or equivalent regulatory partner.** Financial infrastructure. Interest: generate revenue from transaction volume processed. Influence: high in the MVP phase (without it the product cannot operate legally), declining as Ágio structures its own licence.

**Investor (angel in the initial phase, FIDC in the scale phase).** Capital. Interest: financial return with compatible risk. Influence: high during the fundraising phase, modulated by traction milestones. Does not make day-to-day operational decisions, but determines when and how much capital comes in.

There are two secondary stakeholders worth recording: the **Central Bank**, as the segment's regulator, and **payroll ERP providers** (Domínio, Totvs, Senior), as potential integration points in the long term. Neither has active influence in the MVP phase, but both come onto the radar as the model scales.

The relationship between stakeholders is ordered by the value chain: investor finances, QI Tech enables, office distributes, SME authorises, worker uses. A failure at any link stalls the cycle.

## 7. How the business will make money

Ágio's monetisation has a single primary source at the early stage: the fee charged to the worker on each advance. Other revenue sources may be explored in the long term (adjacent financial products, anonymised data revenue, profit sharing with partners), but they are not part of the picture for now.

The fee is fixed per transaction, not a monthly interest rate. The distinction matters for three reasons: simplicity for the user (they know how much they will pay before requesting), transparency (there is no cost that grows over time), and a more favourable regulatory framing (the product positions itself as early access to already-earned wages, not as a loan).

The exact fee amount, the detailed pricing rationale, the market comparison and the full financial projections come in Sprint 2 (pricing and costs) and Sprint 3 (projections and financial indicators). In this foundation, what matters is establishing the structure: Ágio captures revenue from the worker side, passes a portion to the office as recurring commission, and keeps an operating margin on the remainder. The SME pays nothing, the office receives a commission, the worker pays a transparent fee, and Ágio keeps the spread between what comes in and what goes out.

The structurally low default rate (reference: 0.2% at Mêntore Bank with a similar mechanism) is what makes the model financially viable at small volumes. In traditional credit products, scale is necessary to absorb high default rates. At Ágio, the low default rate means the break-even point can be reached with a few hundred to a few thousand active users, depending on the cost structure.

Financial details will come in the next sprints with the depth each one requires.
