# Executive Summary

Ágio is a payroll advance platform for formally employed workers at small and medium-sized businesses, distributed through accounting firms. The product addresses a specific gap: twenty million Brazilians with formal employment contracts and incomes between 1 and 3 minimum wages have no access to emergency credit that isn't a trap. Revolving credit cards charge 451.5% per year. Informal lenders charge 10% to 15% per month. Employers refuse because they aren't equipped to manage credit risk. Ágio fills that gap with a fixed fee of R$ 9.90 per advance — regardless of the amount requested — and automatic deduction from the following month's payroll.

The differentiator isn't the financial product, which is well-established. It's the channel. No player in this segment uses accounting firms as distributors. Ágio signs with one firm and gains access to its entire portfolio — typically 50 to 200 SMEs, with trust already built with each business owner. The firm doesn't switch banks, doesn't change its payroll process, doesn't take on risk. It exports the monthly closing CSV, uploads it to the dashboard, and downloads the deduction report at the end of the cycle. In return, it receives 25% of the revenue generated by its active portfolio — approximately R$ 1,875 per month on a base of 500 active employees with an average frequency of 1.5 advances per month.

The mechanism that makes the model sustainable is automatic payroll deduction, the same one behind Mêntore Bank's 0.2% default rate — the closest comparable operation in Brazil. Near-zero default enables low fees. Low fees enable high usage frequency. The operational break-even is estimated between 9,615 and 14,423 active users, equivalent to 6 to 9 accounting firms at full capacity, with monthly fixed costs of R$ 40,000 to R$ 60,000.

The product is currently in functional MVP demo stage. The Node.js/Express/SQLite backend implements the full cycle — payroll CSV upload, per-employee advance limit calculation, advance requests with business rule validation, deduction report generation, and an employee-facing page accessed via link and CPF. No real financial integration, no actual payments processed. The goal of this phase is to validate the flow and support the academic panel presentation with a navigable product and fictional data.

Three core hypotheses remain unvalidated with primary evidence. No CLT worker has been directly interviewed. No accounting firm has heard the pitch. The actual costs of the financial infrastructure (QI Tech) have not been confirmed — pricing figures use public market benchmarks and may shift within a range. Validation with accountants and end users is scheduled for Sprint 4, when the functional product will be available for demonstration.

Next steps: full financial projections including break-even, IRR, NPV, and ROE in Sprint 3; first conversations with accounting firms and in-depth market analysis in Sprint 4; business plan consolidation and final thesis document in Sprint 5.

# Vision and Mission

The Brazilian financial system operates in layers. At the top, cheap and abundant credit, multiple options competing for the customer. In the middle, moderate credit, accessible to those with a score, collateral, documentation. At the base, a void. Twenty million workers with formal employment contracts, garnishable salaries, documented lives — pushed toward the shadow market every time the month runs short. They pay 10% to 15% a month to informal lenders. They borrow from relatives. They end up credit-blacklisted over an R$ 80 utility bill. Their financial lives are organized around a scarcity that isn't about income — it's about access.

Ágio exists because this configuration isn't inevitable.

## Vision

**That the low-income formally employed Brazilian worker stops being treated as invisible by the financial system.**

This isn't about democratizing credit, a worn-out phrase that lost meaning after a decade of fintechs promising exactly that. It's about a specific and measurable change in the concrete lives of people earning between 1 and 3 minimum wages in Brazil. This worker should be able to cover a R$ 300 emergency without compromising the next paycheck, without having to expose their financial situation to their boss, without having to find an informal lender downtown. They should be able to do it from their phone, in a few taps, paying a fair fee they can read before accepting. That scenario, today, doesn't exist for 20 million people.

The reason to believe this is achievable isn't optimism. It's evidence. Mêntore Bank proves the product works when operating inside large companies. The 2024 Crédito do Trabalhador regulation opened the legal path. Accounting firms across Brazil already have the data, the companies' trust, and the infrastructure to distribute. What's missing is someone connecting these pieces at the company size that nobody wants to serve. That's Ágio's work.

## Mission

**To bring transparent, payroll-deducted wage advances to CLT workers at Brazilian SMEs, through the accounting firms that already serve those businesses.**

Each part of that sentence addresses a specific friction. Wage advance instead of loan, because the worker is accessing money that's already theirs, not taking on new debt. Transparent, because the credit available to this profile today hides costs in compounding monthly interest. Payroll-deducted, because that's the mechanism that keeps default low enough for the model to operate at a fee that doesn't recreate the trap. Distributed through accounting firms, because direct sales to SMEs with 10 to 100 employees has never been economically viable — and it's exactly that unviability that keeps the segment without a solution.

The mission is fulfilled at the operational level, conversation by conversation with accounting firms, business by business that agrees to offer it, advance by advance that goes through without a problem. The vision is fulfilled at the aggregate level, when enough of those operations combined change the landscape of what's available to that worker. One feeds the other. If the mission fails, the vision doesn't happen. If the vision stops making sense, the mission loses direction.

# Problem and Opportunity

## The gap in the middle of the pyramid

The Brazilian financial system works well at the extremes. At the top, abundant credit, competitive rates, multiple products competing for the customer. At the informal base, lenders and loan sharks fill the space banks refuse. In the middle — and this is the segment that matters for Ágio — there's a gap that persists for structural reasons, not for lack of demand.

Twenty million workers with formal employment contracts, incomes between 1 and 3 minimum wages, organized and documented financial lives. IBGE estimates that more than 60% of private-sector employees fall in this range. A 2024 CNDL and SPC Brasil survey found that 6 out of 10 Brazilians couldn't absorb a R$ 1,000 emergency without taking on debt. These workers have garnishable salaries, formal employers, and in many cases clean credit records — but no access to credit that isn't a trap.

When the month runs short, the available options are: revolving credit cards at 451.5% per year (Central Bank, September 2025), overdraft at 134.2% per year, informal lenders at 10% to 15% per month, or asking the boss. The boss, rationally, says no. Fernando Samogin described this in a Module 1 interview: employees asked for advances and he refused — not from lack of empathy, but because lending money to employees creates legal and financial risks a small business isn't equipped to manage.

The result is that a R$ 300 need becomes a debt that erodes months of salary — not because the worker was irresponsible, but because there's no reasonable alternative.

## Why this problem still exists

The answer isn't a lack of products. Payroll-deducted credit exists, wage advance exists, fintechs operate in the segment. The problem is the channel. Mêntore Bank, the most relevant player in the segment in Brazil with R$ 1 billion in projected 2025 revenue and 0.2% default rate, operates exclusively in large companies — and requires a full bank switch as a condition of entry. An insurmountable barrier for a 30-employee SME.

Xerpa, Flash, Caju, and Creditas sell directly to companies. The per-company acquisition cost doesn't work for SMEs with 10 to 100 employees. The segment is ignored by go-to-market structure, not by absence of pain.

That's Ágio's space: serving the segment nobody serves, through a channel nobody uses.

## The channel opportunity

Brazil has more than 102,000 active accounting firms (CRCSP and CFC, January 2026), 29,000 of them in São Paulo. An average firm serves between 50 and 200 SMEs. It already has each business owner's trust, built over years of relationship. It already processes payroll monthly. It already has the salary data that is exactly the input needed to originate payroll-deducted credit.

Activating one firm activates an entire portfolio. The cost of acquisition per end company drops by an order of magnitude. The sales cycle shortens because the firm becomes a recurring distributor with pre-built trust.

No player in the segment uses this channel. That's Ágio's core operational differentiator.

## Recognized gaps

This characterization relies on robust secondary data and three interviews with business owners conducted in Module 1. Primary evidence from the CLT worker side is missing — none were directly interviewed before this sprint closed. The usage hypothesis and willingness to pay R$ 9.90 per advance need to be confirmed with real users. That validation is scheduled for Sprint 4, when the functional product will be available for demonstration.

# Solution and Value Proposition

## What Ágio does

Ágio is a payroll advance platform distributed through accounting firms. The CLT worker receives a link, enters their tax ID (CPF), sees their available balance, and requests the amount they need. The fee is R$ 9.90 per advance, regardless of the amount. The deduction happens automatically in the following month's payroll. No app, no registration, no credit check.

On the accounting firm side: after closing payroll in the system they already use (Domínio, Totvs, Senior), they export the CSV and upload it to the Ágio dashboard. The system calculates each employee's limit, processes the cycle's requests, and generates the deduction report for the following payroll. No existing process is replaced.

## Three product decisions that define the model

**No bank switch.** The differentiator that removes the main adoption barrier for SMEs. Mêntore Bank requires the company to migrate its entire payroll to the partner bank. Ágio enters the existing flow as one additional step, not a replacement. For the SME, the decision to participate carries no operational risk.

**Fixed fee, not monthly interest.** R$ 9.90 per transaction, disclosed before confirmation. The worker knows the cost before requesting, knows when the deduction will happen, knows the transaction closes itself. No cost that grows over time, no surprise in the following statement. That predictability is part of the product, not a communication detail.

**Link with tax ID, no app.** Apps require downloads, phone storage, data plans. The target audience uses basic phones with limited storage. The link with CPF resolves access and identification in a single step, without friction. A UX decision with direct impact on adoption rate.

## Value proposition by segment

**For the CLT worker:** access to part of their own salary before payday, without a credit check, without Serasa, with a predictable fee significantly lower than the available alternatives. R$ 9.90 against R$ 25 to R$ 30 on revolving credit for R$ 200 over 30 days. The product doesn't create new debt — it advances money that has already been earned.

**For the accounting firm:** a new recurring revenue stream on an existing portfolio, with no onboarding cost, no credit risk, no process change. The 25% commission on each transaction generates revenue proportional to the size of the active portfolio. With 500 employees at a base frequency of 1.5 advances per month, the firm receives approximately R$ 1,875 in additional monthly revenue.

**For the SME:** a real benefit for employees — reduced financial stress, lower turnover in base-level roles — with no cost, no risk, no bureaucracy. The company doesn't pay, doesn't guarantee, doesn't interfere. It authorizes once and the product runs.

## The mechanism that makes the model sustainable

Automatic payroll deduction is what makes operating with a low fee viable. In traditional credit models, high default requires high rates to absorb the risk. In Ágio, the deduction happens before the money reaches the worker's account — the same mechanism behind Mêntore Bank's documented 0.2% default rate. Near-zero default enables low fees. Low fees enable high usage frequency. High frequency makes the unit economics work even in small portfolios.

The operational break-even is estimated between 9,615 and 14,423 active users — equivalent to 6 to 9 accounting firms at full capacity. Full margin, cost, and break-even detail is in the Pricing section.

## What has been built

The functional MVP demo developed in this sprint implements the full cycle in a controlled environment with fictional data: accounting firm panel with login, company registration, CSV upload, employee list with business-rule-calculated limits, individual detail view with advance history, deduction report generation, and an employee-facing page with CPF access, advance requests, and history. Node.js/Express/SQLite backend, HTML/CSS/JS frontend. No external integrations, no real payments — the goal is to validate the flow and demonstrate the proposal to the academic panel.

# Pricing

Pricing is where the business plan stops being strategy and becomes arithmetic. Every number from here on answers a concrete question: how much Ágio will charge the worker, how much it will pass to the accounting firm, and what's left to cover costs and generate margin. The three variables influence each other — moving one shifts the others.

This chapter follows a simple sequence: what the market does today, with public sources; the direct costs of Ágio's operation, with the numbers open and caveats where they exist; the commission analysis for the accounting firm, with tested variations; and finally the fee charged to the worker, with the remaining margin and the operational break-even.

One caveat before starting. The unit cost of the financial infrastructure Ágio will contract (most likely QI Tech, possibly Celcoin) isn't published. A formal email was sent to QI Tech requesting their pricing table and all information needed to use the service. The response hasn't arrived. The numbers here use public market benchmarks for equivalent services (business Pix, typical BaaS costs, FIDC capital cost), and can be adjusted when QI Tech figures are confirmed. The analytical method doesn't change. The absolute values may shift within a range.

## What the market charges today

To assess whether Ágio's fee is high or low, you need to know what's actually available to the target worker today. The analysis covers three groups: the expensive traditional credit they turn to when they have no other option, salary advance fintechs operating in larger markets, and benefits offered by the employer.

**Expensive traditional credit.** Per Central Bank data published by Agência Brasil in September 2025, revolving credit cards run at 451.5% per year for individuals, equivalent to approximately 15% per month [1]. Overdraft is at 134.2% per year [2]. Unsecured personal credit is at 106.6% per year [3]. These products are theoretically available to any account holder, but in practice the base-level CLT worker — who tends to be credit-blacklisted — only accesses revolving credit (when they already have a card) and overdraft (when the bank allows it). In absolute terms, a worker who needs R$ 200 and uses revolving credit for 25 days pays approximately R$ 25 in interest. Over a full 30 days, R$ 30.

**Informal lenders.** There's no official data on rates charged by informal lenders and easy-access loan apps for blacklisted borrowers. Reporting and user interviews converge on 10% to 15% per month, putting R$ 200 borrowed for 30 days at R$ 20 to R$ 30 in interest [4]. The additional risk is that a significant portion of these loans roll over, and the debt grows month by month when unpaid.

**Salary advance fintechs charging the worker.** Xerpa, per a NeoFeed report from September 2020, charged a fixed fee of R$ 5 per advance, with an average ticket of R$ 350 [5]. The model operates inside large companies (iFood, Mercado Livre, CCR, Nubank), with high volume that makes low fees viable. Sólides, through its partner Payssego, charges a fixed fee of R$ 14.90 per request, with Pix payment in seconds — a model very close to Ágio's [6].

**Fintechs operating as a free benefit for the worker.** Caju, Flash, and UpGo offer salary advances with no fee to the worker. The cost is absorbed by the contracting company, which pays a monthly fee or per-active-employee charge [7]. This model is incompatible with Ágio, because it depends on the company paying for the benefit — which brings back the problem of direct sales to small SMEs that isn't economically viable.

Reading these data points together establishes a clear reference range. The target worker today pays between R$ 20 and R$ 30 in interest to access R$ 200 over 30 days through expensive credit products. Competing fintechs that charge a fixed fee operate between R$ 5 (Xerpa, at large scale) and R$ 14.90 (Sólides). Ágio's positioning needs to land between those extremes.

## Direct costs per transaction

Each advance processed by Ágio generates five categories of direct cost. The values presented are estimates based on public market benchmarks, subject to revision when QI Tech information is confirmed.

**Cost of capital.** When a worker advances R$ 200, Ágio needs those R$ 200 available to send. That capital sits idle until the payroll deduction the following month, on average 25 days. In the validation phase, capital may come from the founder or an angel investor. At scale, it comes from a FIDC. Senior FIDC costs, per Hike Capital and Stone data, run between CDI + 2% and CDI + 3% per year [8][9]. With Brazil's benchmark rate (Selic) at 15% per year in 2025, the total cost is approximately 18% per year. For R$ 200 over 25 days: R$ 200 × 0.18 × (25/365) = **R$ 2.46**.

**Pix transfer.** Business Pix is charged by financial institutions. Per a Paytime report from March 2026, rates range from R$ 0.50 to R$ 1.50 per transaction for legal entities, depending on the provider and volume [10]. Working with the conservative average of **R$ 1.00 per transaction**.

**Backend processing and cloud infrastructure.** The cost of keeping the application running — database, servers, auxiliary tools — varies significantly with volume. At initial scale, the per-transaction allocation is around **R$ 0.40**, based on typical early-stage fintech estimates. This number decreases as volume grows.

**KYC and fraud prevention.** CPF validation (Federal Revenue query), transactional fraud prevention, and bank data validation are per-transaction services. QI Tech offers these integrated into its platform, and the typical market cost is around **R$ 0.30 per transaction**.

**Prorated support and operations.** Accounting firm support, worker support for questions, manual adjustments, and exception handling. In the validation phase, this cost is higher because volume is low and each case requires human attention. Estimate of **R$ 0.50 per transaction** in the validation phase, declining with volume.

**Total direct costs per transaction: R$ 4.66.**

This number aggregates five sources of uncertainty. The most significant is the cost of capital, which depends on the Selic rate and the FIDC structure when it exists. The other four categories are more predictable but depend on QI Tech's pricing table, which hasn't been confirmed. In a pessimistic estimate (Pix R$ 1.50, support R$ 0.80, capital with higher Selic), direct costs could reach R$ 6. In an optimistic estimate (Pix R$ 0.50, support R$ 0.30, falling Selic), they could drop to R$ 3.80. The reasonable working range is R$ 4 to R$ 5.50 per transaction.

## Commission paid to the accounting firm

The commission is the variable that determines whether the firm agrees to offer the product. Higher means more attractive to the channel. Lower means more room for Ágio to retain margin. Four scenarios tested, assuming a hypothetical fixed fee of R$ 10 charged to the worker, average frequency of 1.5 advances per month per active user, and a reference portfolio of 500 active employees.

**15% commission.** R$ 1.50 per transaction. At 500 active employees × 1.5 uses/month, the firm receives approximately R$ 1,125 per month in recurring revenue. Not attractive to mid-size or large firms, who see that revenue as marginal. May work for small firms looking for any additional revenue stream.

**20% commission.** R$ 2.00 per transaction. Same base, R$ 1,500 per month. The original plan figure. Reasonable, but not exceptional. The firm agrees, but doesn't become an evangelist for the product.

**25% commission.** R$ 2.50 per transaction. R$ 1,875 per month on the same base. Significantly more attractive, especially when the firm projects what that represents across a full portfolio of 50 to 200 companies. Starts to approach the average billing for a small SME accounting client, which makes the proposal concrete.

**30% commission.** R$ 3.00 per transaction. R$ 2,250 per month. Very attractive to the firm, but Ágio retains R$ 7 per transaction against direct costs of R$ 4.66. Gross margin drops to R$ 2.34 per transaction, which compresses the ability to cover fixed costs and generate profit.

**Recommendation: 25% commission.** Three reasons. First, it's more attractive than the original 20%, which may accelerate channel adoption at a stage where adoption is the metric that matters most. Second, it maintains healthy gross margin for Ágio (R$ 7.50 retained against R$ 4.66 in direct costs, leaving R$ 2.84 per transaction). Third, it positions the product as genuinely shared between Ágio and the channel, which differentiates it from models where the channel's cut is seen as symbolic.

## Usage frequency scenarios

Usage frequency per active user is a variable outside Ágio's direct control. It depends on the worker's financial reality and behavior after the first advance. Three scenarios, all anchored in public data from similar operations.

**Conservative scenario: 1.0 advance per month.** Assumes the user accesses once a month for a one-off emergency and doesn't build a habit. Consistent with a population without recurring financial pain — only isolated events.

**Base scenario: 1.5 advances per month.** Consistent with what Mêntore Bank documented in actual operation and with the average suggested by Roberto Amaral, QI Tech's head of lending, in a NeoFeed interview from February 2026 on payroll-deducted credit as a service [11]. Represents the user who has incorporated the product into their financial routine.

**Optimistic scenario: 2.0 advances per month.** Consistent with what Xerpa reported in 2020, with 30% adoption and recurring use in some companies. Represents an engaged portfolio with a well-communicated product.

These three scenarios feed the revenue, margin, and break-even calculations in the following sections.

## Fee charged to the worker

With direct costs estimated at R$ 4.66 per transaction and a 25% commission passed to the accounting firm, the fee needs to cover both and still generate enough margin for the business's fixed costs. Three values tested.

**R$ 8 per advance.** Ágio's net revenue (R$ 8 minus 25% commission) = R$ 6. Gross margin after direct costs = R$ 1.34. Represents 4% of the R$ 200 average ticket. Very aggressive market positioning, below Sólides (R$ 14.90) and most competitors that charge the worker — still cheaper than revolving credit by an order of magnitude. Per-transaction margin is tight, and the operational break-even rises significantly.

**R$ 9.90 per advance.** Ágio's net revenue = R$ 7.43. Gross margin = R$ 2.77. Represents 4.95% of the average ticket. Still well below what the target population pays today on revolving credit (R$ 25 to R$ 30 for R$ 200 over 30 days). The amount ending in 9.90 reads as "under R$ 10" and carries lighter psychological weight.

**R$ 12 per advance.** Net revenue = R$ 9. Gross margin = R$ 4.34. Represents 6% of the average ticket. Closer to Sólides (R$ 14.90) and still well below expensive credit. Comfortable margin, but the user starts to feel the weight of the fee at lower ticket sizes (at R$ 150, R$ 12 represents 8%, which may affect the decision to use again).

**Recommendation: R$ 9.90 per advance.** Balances three objectives. First, keeps the product visibly cheaper than the alternatives the target population faces today (R$ 9.90 against R$ 25 on revolving credit is a 60% difference). Second, generates enough gross margin to sustain the business at a reasonable scale of active users. Third, lands in a memorable price range, below the psychological barrier of double digits.

## Margin and break-even

With a R$ 9.90 fee, 25% commission, R$ 4.66 in direct costs, and a base frequency of 1.5 advances per month per active user, Ágio's unit economics look like this:

- Gross revenue per transaction: R$ 9.90
- Commission to the accounting firm (25%): R$ 2.48
- Ágio's net revenue: R$ 7.43
- Direct transaction cost: R$ 4.66
- **Gross margin per transaction: R$ 2.77** (37% of net revenue)
- **Monthly net revenue per active user (base scenario, 1.5 uses/month): R$ 11.15**
- **Monthly gross margin per active user: R$ 4.16**

To reach operating margin, fixed business costs must be covered: salaries for the lean initial team (3 to 5 people), software, administrative infrastructure, accounting firm acquisition marketing, legal, and accounting. Monthly fixed cost estimate in the validation phase: **R$ 40,000 to R$ 60,000**, depending on team size and marketing volume.

With a monthly gross margin of R$ 4.16 per active user, the operational break-even falls between **9,615 and 14,423 active users**, depending on which end of the fixed cost range materializes. In terms of partner accounting firms, assuming an average portfolio of 1,500 to 2,500 active employees per well-developed firm, this represents **6 to 9 firms** at full capacity.

This calculation is consistent with the original Module 1 estimate, which pointed to break-even at around 6 firms. The current analysis confirms the order of magnitude with greater depth across the variables.

To close the pricing argument, it helps to put Ágio on the same scale as the other products available to the target population. For a R$ 200 advance over a 30-day cycle, the user pays approximately:

|---------|----------------------|-------|
| Revolving credit card | R$ 30 | Central Bank, September 2025 [1] |
| Overdraft | R$ 22 | Central Bank, April 2025 [2] |

It sits above Xerpa, but Xerpa operates at much larger scale inside large companies — a context that significantly reduces unit costs. Ágio's R$ 9.90 positioning is defensible within the bounds of this market.

## Caveats and future adjustments

Three points of the pricing presented depend on external information that has not yet been confirmed and may shift the absolute numbers without changing the underlying logic.

First, QI Tech's pricing table (or that of whichever financial infrastructure is chosen) has not been received. The email sent requests not just the table, but also contractual terms, regulatory requirements, integration timelines, and all other information needed to use the service. When the response arrives, direct costs will be revised with real figures rather than benchmarks.

Second, the cost of capital depends on the current Selic rate and the specific FIDC structure when it is set up. In the initial validation phase, capital may come from other sources (founder capital, angel investor, partner fintech credit line), at different costs. The FIDC enters in the scale phase, with a typical structuring window of six to twelve months.

Third, the 25% commission may be adjusted up or down based on feedback from the first accounting firms contacted in Sprint 4. If the commission proves insufficient to accelerate adoption, it warrants revision. If it's seen as excessive, recalibration is in order.

The pricing presented reflects what makes sense with the data available today. It is a defensible starting point, not an immutable decision. The purpose of this chapter is to demonstrate the analytical method and the business viability range, more than to lock in absolute numbers.

## Development cost

Ágio's development cost breaks into two phases with very different profiles: the validation phase, corresponding to the functional MVP demo, and the live operation phase, when the product requires regulated financial infrastructure and a dedicated team.

### Validation phase — functional MVP demo

The MVP was built on a deliberately lean stack to minimize cost and dependence on external infrastructure. Node.js with Express, SQLite, plain HTML/CSS/JS, no frontend framework, no payment service, no robust authentication. Development cost in this phase is primarily a time cost, not a hiring or tooling cost.

| Item | Estimated cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Development (own time) | R$ 0 direct | Estimated 80 to 120 hours of active development across the sprint |
| Hosting and infrastructure | R$ 0 to R$ 50/month | Render, Railway, or equivalent for Node deployment; SQLite requires no managed database |
| Domain | R$ 40 to R$ 80/year | Optional for MVP — localhost works for demo |
| Development tools | R$ 0 | VS Code, GitLab, Postman — all free |
| AI assistance (Claude Pro) | R$ 110/month | Pro Max plan — actively used for MVP development, modeling, documentation, and code generation |
| **Validation phase total** | **R$ 110 to R$ 240/month** | Dominated by AI and hosting costs |

The real cost of the validation phase combines the opportunity cost of time invested and the direct cost of AI tooling. At 80 to 120 hours per sprint of active development, assuming a junior-to-mid developer at market rates of R$ 50 to R$ 80 per hour, the implicit development cost of the MVP falls between **R$ 4,000 and R$ 9,600**. Adding Claude Pro Max (R$ 110/month), the direct tooling cost runs around **R$ 330 to R$ 720 over a three-to-six-month validation period** — marginal relative to time cost, but not negligible, and worth documenting because it represents a structural shift in how the product was built: a significant portion of the modeling, documentation, code generation, and business analysis was produced with AI assistance, which compresses development time and reduces the total implicit cost.

### Live operation phase — pre-scale

The transition from MVP to a live product requires four structural changes, each with an associated cost.

**Financial infrastructure (BaaS).** Integrating with QI Tech or Celcoin to process real advances, Pix transfers, and FIDC management is not free. The technical integration cost involves development time (estimated 2 to 4 weeks from a developer experienced with financial APIs) plus the per-transaction fees already detailed in the direct costs section. QI Tech publishes no monthly fixed cost — it depends on volume negotiation.

**Authentication and security.** Replacing the mock login with real authentication (JWT, OAuth, or equivalent), encrypting CPF and sensitive data, and meeting minimum LGPD compliance represents approximately **40 to 60 additional development hours**.

**Managed database.** The MVP's SQLite doesn't scale to multiple accounting firms running simultaneously in production. Migration to PostgreSQL on a managed service (Supabase, Railway, Render) costs between **R$ 0 and R$ 150/month** at initial usage tiers.

**Minimum team to operate.** Ágio doesn't run itself after the MVP. The minimum structure to serve the first accounting firms without the founder handling everything manually includes:

| Role | Arrangement | Estimated cost/month |
|---|---|---|
| Fullstack developer (1) | Junior CLT or contractor | R$ 5,000 to R$ 8,000 |
| Support / accounting firm onboarding (1) | Part-time or contractor | R$ 2,000 to R$ 3,500 |
| **Minimum team total** | | **R$ 7,000 to R$ 11,500/month** |

Adding infrastructure and team, the **monthly fixed cost in the live operation phase falls between R$ 7,150 and R$ 11,650**, consistent with the total fixed cost estimate of R$ 40,000 to R$ 60,000 presented in the margin and break-even section — the difference being that the larger estimate covers more advanced phases with a bigger team, acquisition marketing, and legal and accounting structure.

### Total cost to operational break-even

Combining MVP development cost, transition to live operation, and monthly fixed costs until break-even (estimated at 6 to 9 active accounting firms, over a 12 to 18-month horizon), the total cost to the break-even point falls in the following range:

| Phase | Estimated duration | Estimated cost |
|---|---|---|
| MVP and validation | 3 to 6 months | R$ 5,000 to R$ 15,000 |
| Live pre-scale operation | 6 to 12 months | R$ 85,000 to R$ 140,000 |
| **Total to break-even** | **9 to 18 months** | **R$ 90,000 to R$ 155,000** |

This figure represents the capital needed to take Ágio from the current MVP to the point where the operation sustains itself. It is compatible with an angel investment round or founder capital with savings, and significantly below what would be required under a model that demanded a bank switch or native app development.

### Caveats

The figures in this section are estimates based on 2026 market prices for services and professionals in Brazil. Actual cost depends on three still-open variables: QI Tech's pricing table (which affects per-transaction cost and possibly integration cost), the pace of accounting firm acquisition (which determines the speed to break-even), and the decision between hiring a dedicated team versus operating with freelancers and automation in the early stages.

**ESCRITORIO**
The accounting firm. It is the system's entry point — it registers with Ágio and manages everything from there. Each firm has its own CNPJ (company tax ID) and an access email.

**EMPRESA**
The SME client of the accounting firm. It does not register itself — the firm registers it (`escritorio_id` as a foreign key). A company belongs to exactly one firm; a firm can have many companies.

**FUNCIONARIO**
The formally employed worker. Belongs to a company (`empresa_id`). The `salario_liquido` field defines the advance ceiling (40%). `data_admissao` is used to apply the reduced ceiling for workers with less than 3 months on the job. `status` (active/inactive) allows deactivating terminated employees without deleting their advance history.

**CICLO_FOLHA**
Represents one CSV upload per company. When the accountant closes a month's payroll and uploads the file to Ágio, a record is created here. `competencia` is the month/year of reference (e.g., `2026-05`). `status` indicates whether the file has been processed or is pending. This entity exists because advance deductions need to be anchored to a specific payroll cycle — without it, there's no way to know which payroll will deduct a given advance.

**ANTECIPACAO**
The core of the product. Every advance request generates a record. It has two foreign keys: `funcionario_id` (who requested it) and `ciclo_folha_id` (which payroll will deduct it). This allows a worker to have more than one open advance at the same time — each linked to the correct cycle. `valor` is the amount advanced, `taxa` is the R$ 9.90 fee charged. `status` follows: `approved → deducted → cancelled`.

---

## Relationships

| Relationship | Cardinality | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ESCRITORIO → EMPRESA | 1 to many | One firm manages multiple companies |
| EMPRESA → FUNCIONARIO | 1 to many | One company has multiple employees |
| EMPRESA → CICLO_FOLHA | 1 to many | One company generates one cycle per payroll closing |
| FUNCIONARIO → ANTECIPACAO | 1 to many | One employee can have multiple advances (simultaneous or historical) |
| CICLO_FOLHA → ANTECIPACAO | 1 to many | One cycle groups all advances to be deducted in that payroll |

---

## Why this structure

The non-obvious point is the existence of `CICLO_FOLHA` as a separate entity. The alternative would be to store just a date in `ANTECIPACAO`. The problem: when the accountant generates the deduction report, they need all of a company's advances for a closed period — not scattered dates. With `CICLO_FOLHA`, the query is trivial: `WHERE ciclo_folha_id = X`. Without it, every report becomes a date filter with room for error.

The other relevant decision is `FUNCIONARIO.status`. Terminated employees can't be deleted while they have open or historical advances. The field resolves this without violating referential integrity.


## Reading the diagram

**System boundary** is the dashed rectangle. Everything inside is the responsibility of Ágio. Everything outside are actors — entities that interact with the system but are not part of it.

**Actors** are represented by stick figures. An actor is any external entity that initiates or participates in an interaction with the system. In Ágio there are two:

- **Accountant**: operates the office panel. They are the one who registers companies, uploads files and generates reports. They are the primary actor on the operational side.
- **CLT Employee**: accesses the page via link + CPF. They are the primary actor on the consumer side.

**Use cases** are the ellipses. Each one represents a complete feature from the actor's point of view — not an internal technical step, but something with observable value to whoever is using it.

**Association lines** connect the actor to the use case they can initiate. They have no arrowhead — in UML, a simple association is a directionless line.


## The use cases

> **![\[use-cases.png\]](https://res.cloudinary.com/dloj4cl3v/image/upload/v1778868198/diagrama-casos-de-uso_bwt879.jpg)**

**From the Accountant:**

`Authenticate` — the accountant enters credentials on the panel. In the MVP it is a fake login that accepts any input, but the use case exists because in production there will be real authentication.

`Register company` — the accountant registers an SME in the system, linking it to their office. From that point, the employees of that company become available in the panel.

`Upload payroll` — after closing the payroll in their own system (Domínio, Totvs, Senior), the accountant exports the CSV and uploads it to Ágio. The system processes the file, updates net salaries and defines the advance limit for each employee for that cycle.

`View panel` — list of employees of the selected company, with status, available balance and advance history. It is the accountant's main operating screen.

`Generate report` — exports a CSV or PDF with all advances from that company's cycle, ready to be posted as a deduction on next month's payroll.

**From the CLT Employee:**

`Access by CPF` — the employee receives a link from the company (or from the accountant) and logs in by entering their CPF. No registration, no password. The authentication is the CPF itself linked to the record imported via CSV.

`Request advance` — chooses the amount within the available limit (up to 40% of net salary) and confirms. The system records the advance with status `pending`, which moves to `approved` and then to `deducted` in the next payroll cycle.

`Check history` — views all previous advances: amount, date, status and fee charged. Transparency about what has already been requested and what is still outstanding.

## What the diagram does not show

`<<include>>` relationships were left out to avoid clutter. Two of them exist and are worth documenting:

- `Upload payroll` includes `Process and validate CSV` — when the file is uploaded, the system validates required fields, detects CPFs with no binding record and calculates the ceiling for each employee. This happens automatically and is not an actor action.
- `Request advance` includes `Calculate available limit` — before displaying the available amounts, the system sums the outstanding advances and subtracts them from the employee's ceiling. The employee does not directly initiate this calculation.

These two `<<include>>` relationships will appear in the test scenarios, which is the next document.

## Conventions

Each use case follows this structure:

- **Actor**: who initiates the interaction
- **Pre-conditions**: what must be true before the start
- **Post-conditions**: system state after successful execution
- **Main flow**: happy path, step by step
- **Alternative flows (AF)**: expected and recoverable deviations
- **Exception flows (EF)**: failures that interrupt the flow
- **Test scenarios**: concrete cases for validation

Test scenarios use the **Given / When / Then** format.  
The *MVP* column indicates whether the behavior is implemented in the functional demo or only specified for production.

## UC03 — Upload payroll

**Actor:** Accountant  
**Pre-conditions:** Accountant authenticated; company registered in the system.  
**Post-conditions:** `PAYROLL_CYCLE` created for the company; `EMPLOYEE` records created or updated; advance ceiling for each employee calculated based on the imported `net_salary`.

### Main flow

1. Accountant selects a company in the panel.
2. Accountant accesses the payroll upload option.
3. System displays a file selector and a competency field (month/year).
4. Accountant enters the competency (e.g. `2026-05`) and selects the CSV file exported from the payroll system.
5. Accountant confirms the submission.
6. System validates the file format (extension and expected columns).
7. System processes each row: creates or updates the `EMPLOYEE` record with `cpf`, `name`, `net_salary` and `hire_date`.
8. System creates the `PAYROLL_CYCLE` record with status `processed`.
9. System displays a summary: total employees imported, new vs. updated, alerts for CPFs with issues.

### Alternative flows

**AF03.A — CPF in the file not found as an active employee**  
At step 7, for each row with a new CPF:  
7a. System creates a new `EMPLOYEE` record with status `active`.  
7b. Continues processing the remaining rows.

**AF03.B — Existing employee with changed data**  
At step 7, if the CPF already exists but `net_salary` has changed:  
7b. System updates the field. Outstanding advances prior to the upload are not affected.

**AF03.C — Competency already processed for the company**  
At step 6, if a `PAYROLL_CYCLE` with the same competency already exists for this company:  
6a. System warns: "A payroll has already been processed for this period. Do you want to replace it?"  
6b. If confirmed, the previous cycle is marked as inactive and the new one is created.  
6c. If cancelled, the flow ends with no changes.

**AF03.D — Rows with missing required fields**  
At step 7, for each row without a CPF or without `net_salary`:  
7a. System skips the row and records it in the summary as "skipped (incomplete data)".  
7b. Continues processing the remaining rows.

### Exception flows

**EF03.A — File is not a CSV**  
At step 6, if the extension is not `.csv`:  
6a. System rejects the file and displays: "Invalid format. Please upload a .csv file."  
6b. Returns to step 4.

**EF03.B — Expected columns missing from CSV**  
At step 6, if the CSV file does not contain the columns `cpf`, `name`, `net_salary`, `hire_date`:  
6a. System rejects it and displays which columns are missing.  
6b. Returns to step 4.

**EF03.C — Empty file**  
At step 6, if the file contains no data rows beyond the header:  
6a. System displays: "The file is empty."  
6b. No `PAYROLL_CYCLE` is created.

## UC07 — Request advance

**Actor:** CLT Employee  
**Pre-conditions:** Employee authenticated via CPF; has an available balance greater than zero.  
**Post-conditions:** `ADVANCE` record created with status `approved`; employee's available balance reduced by the requested amount; R$ 9.90 fee recorded.

### Main flow

1. Employee views the available balance on the page.
2. Employee selects the option to request an advance.
3. System displays the form with:
   - Available balance
   - Amount field (numeric, with a minimum of R$ 50.00)
   - Fee summary: R$ 9.90
   - Total amount to receive = requested amount (the fee is deducted from the payroll, not from the amount received)
4. Employee enters the desired amount and confirms.
5. System validates the amount against the current available balance.
6. The system records the advance directly with status approved. There is no credit approval step in the MVP — validation happens before confirmation. The status evolves to deducted after the cycle closes.
7. System displays confirmation: "Advance of R$ X.XX requested. The amount will be deposited within 1 business day."

# User journey — Ágio

## Overview

Ágio has two actors with distinct journeys that intersect at one specific point: the payroll upload. Before that point, only the accountant operates the system. After it, the employee gains access. This dependency is not technical by accident — it reflects the business logic: the system can only calculate a worker's advance ceiling after it knows the net salary for that cycle.

The journey diagram is organised in three horizontal phases and two vertical swim lanes, one per actor.

## Phase 1 — Preparation

**Who acts:** the Accountant only.

The cycle begins when the accounting office closes the payroll in whichever system they already use — Domínio, Totvs, Senior or any other. This closing happens outside Ágio. The accountant exports the CSV file generated by the payroll system and accesses Ágio.

### Step 1 · Login

The accountant enters their email and password on the office panel. The system returns the data for the office linked to that email and starts the session. From that point on, all of the accountant's actions are contextualised within their own office — they only see the companies they themselves registered.

## Phase 2 — In progress

**Who acts:** Accountant (start of phase) and CLT Employee (after activation).

### Step 2 · CSV payroll upload — activation point

The accountant selects the company, enters the cycle competency (e.g. `2026-05`) and uploads the CSV exported from the payroll system. The file must contain four columns: `cpf`, `name`, `net_salary` and `hire_date`.

The system processes the file row by row:

- For each CPF already registered in the company, it updates `net_salary` and `hire_date`.
- For each new CPF, it creates an `EMPLOYEE` record with status `active`.
- Rows without a CPF or without a salary are skipped and counted in the summary.
- A `PAYROLL_CYCLE` record is created with the entered competency and status `processed`.
- If a cycle with the same competency already existed for that company, it is marked as `inactive` before the new one is created.

With the `PAYROLL_CYCLE` created, the system has everything it needs to calculate each employee's ceiling: `net_salary` for the current cycle, `hire_date` to determine whether the full ceiling (40%) or the reduced ceiling (20%, for employees with fewer than 90 days of tenure) applies, and the outstanding advance history to calculate the available balance.

**This is the activation point.** Before the upload, no employee can request an advance — there is no active `PAYROLL_CYCLE` to anchor the transaction. After the upload, all employees imported in that cycle can access the system.

**Screen:** 3 (company panel, upload modal)

### Step 3 · Employee receives the access link

The employee receives a link generated by the office or the company. The link contains the company identifier encoded in the URL (`?company_id=X`). This link can be sent via WhatsApp, email or posted on an internal noticeboard — the channel is not managed by Ágio.

The employee does not need to create an account. There is no registration. Their CPF is already in the system because it was imported via CSV in the previous step.

**External to the system.**

---

### Step 4 · Access by CPF

The employee opens the link in the browser on their phone or computer. The system displays the name of the company linked to the URL and a single input field: the CPF.

The employee enters the CPF. The system locates the record by CPF combined with the `company_id` from the URL. If the CPF does not exist for that company, it returns an error without revealing whether the CPF exists in another context. If the employee is inactive, it returns an access disabled message. If they are active, the session is started and the main page is displayed.

**Screen:** 5 (CPF access)

---

### Step 5 · Advance request

The main page prominently displays the employee's available balance. The balance is calculated in real time: the cycle ceiling minus the sum of all advances with status `approved` in the active cycle. Below the balance is the request button.

The employee enters the desired amount. The system validates:

1. Is the amount greater than or equal to R$ 50.00?
2. Does the amount not exceed the available balance calculated at the time of confirmation — not at the time the form was opened?
3. Is the employee still active?
4. Does an active `PAYROLL_CYCLE` still exist for the company?

If all validations pass, the system creates the `ADVANCE` record with status `approved`, linked to the employee and the active `PAYROLL_CYCLE`. The R$ 9.90 fee is recorded separately from the amount — it will be deducted from the payroll together with the advanced amount, not subtracted from the deposit.

The employee may make more than one advance in the same cycle, as long as there is available balance. Each advance generates a separate record with its own fee.

**Screens:** 6 (dashboard with balance) and 7 (request form)

### Step 6 · History check

At any point during the cycle, the employee can access the complete advance history. Records are grouped by competency and ordered from most recent to oldest. Each record displays the amount advanced, the fee charged, the request date and the current status.

The possible statuses are:

- `approved`: the advance has been recorded and is awaiting the end of the cycle to be deducted.
- `deducted`: the deduction has already been processed by the accountant in a previous cycle.
- `cancelled`: the advance was cancelled before the cycle closed.

The history is not filtered by the active cycle — the employee sees their entire history with the company, which includes previous cycles with advances already deducted.

**Screen:** 8 (history)

## Phase 3 — Cycle closing

**Who acts:** the Accountant only.

At the end of the cycle — typically at the end of the month, when the payroll is closed — the accountant returns to Ágio.

### Step 7 · Panel monitoring

Throughout the entire period in which employees can request advances, the accountant has visibility into the panel. The employee list shows, for each person, the updated available ceiling, the total advanced in the cycle and the status (`no advance`, `advance outstanding` or `limit exhausted`). The accountant can click on any employee to see the complete history with each transaction and its individual status.

This monitoring requires no action from the accountant — it is pure read access. But it serves as a control instrument before generating the final report.

**Screens:** 3 (employee list) and 4 (employee detail)

### Step 8 · Generating the deduction report

With the cycle closed, the accountant clicks "Generate report" on the company panel. The system groups all advances with status `approved` for that `PAYROLL_CYCLE` and generates a CSV file with the following columns:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| `name` | Employee name |
| `cpf` | Employee CPF |
| `amount` | Amount advanced |
| `fee` | R$ 9.90 per advance |
| `total_to_deduct` | amount + fee |
| `request_date` | Request date |

The accountant downloads the file and posts each row as a deduction on the payroll, exactly like any other deduction — health plan, commuter benefit, payroll loan. Ágio does not interfere with the client's payroll system. This is the product's core operational differentiator: it fits into the existing workflow without replacing anything.

After posting the deductions, the accountant can manually update the status of advances to `deducted` — or this can be automated in future versions when API integration with payroll systems becomes available.

**Screen:** 3 (company panel, "Generate report" button)

## Connection between the journeys

The point at which the journeys meet is unique and asymmetric: the payroll upload made by the accountant is a necessary condition for any employee to access the system in that cycle. There is no reverse path — the employee has no way to trigger the accountant, receives no notifications and cannot see what happens in the office panel.

This asymmetry is intentional. Ágio is not a collaboration product between accountant and employee. It is a product where the accountant enables and the employee consumes. The separation of contexts — a desktop web panel for the accountant, a mobile-first page for the employee — reflects exactly this.

## Open gaps

- **Employee notification:** today the employee depends on receiving the link manually. There is no automatic notification when the payroll upload is processed. This is a real friction point that will only be addressed after validation with real users.
- **Status update to `deducted`:** the system records `approved` but does not automatically update to `deducted` after the cycle closes. The manual flow is acceptable in the MVP but will need to be resolved in production.
- **Communication of the reduced ceiling:** the system applies 20% for employees with fewer than 90 days of tenure, but does not show the employee why their balance is lower than expected. This can cause confusion and requires UX treatment.
